# Early Stopping vs. Cosine LR Schedules — Survey

*Research-agent report, 2026-06-09. Web-verified against primary sources. Focused companion to `epoch-budgets-and-lr-scheduling.md` (Chinchilla cosine-horizon, WSD, rewind-to-best) and `ssl-stopping-and-lr-schedules.md` (Hägele cooldown, SSL monitoring). **Does not** re-cover WSD mechanics, El-Nouby constant-iterations, or how the epoch number is picked — see those. This report answers one question: how do **early stopping** and **cosine schedules** interact, and what does that imply for option A (fixed-horizon cosine + best-val checkpoint) vs option B (constant/WSD + best-val + cooldown). Tags: [established] = stated/measured in a primary source; [folklore] = widespread practice, no published proof; [inference] = my reasoning from established facts, not directly stated; [open] = no evidence found.*

## Q1. The cosine-horizon result: you must decay to the predefined end

**The cosine schedule's quality is concentrated at the END of its scheduled decay; an early snapshot of an unfinished cosine is markedly worse than a model whose cosine was *scheduled* to finish at that earlier step.** This is the best-established LR fact relevant here.

- **Loshchilov & Hutter, SGDR** (arXiv:1608.03983, §3): origin of cosine annealing. LR follows `η_t = η_min + ½(η_max−η_min)(1+cos(π·T_cur/T_i))`, decaying smoothly from `η_max` to `η_min` (≈0) over a cycle of length `T_i`. The paper's *whole motivation* for **warm restarts** is that a monotone cosine gives a good model only at the cycle END; restarts produce *multiple* usable endpoints to improve "anytime" performance — i.e. it solves the "no good intermediate model" problem by repeatedly *completing* short cosines, not by reading off a mid-cosine point [established]. Note SGDR proposes restarts as an *improvement* technique; it does **not** claim a half-finished cosine is a good model — the opposite.
- **Hoffmann et al., Chinchilla** (arXiv:2203.15556, **Appendix B "Optimal cosine cycle length," Fig. A1**): with a 10× cosine decay, *"setting the cosine cycle length too much longer than the target number of training steps results in sub-optimally trained models"*; the cycle length must be set ≈ the number of training steps to get the compute-optimal loss [established]. The corollary is exactly the early-stop case: a cosine aimed at horizon T but read off at t<T has only partially decayed, so at step-count t it is *under-annealed* relative to a cosine correctly horizoned to t — strictly worse at that compute. This is the single most-cited statement of the effect.
- **Hägele et al.** (arXiv:2405.18392, §2) restate it as WSD's motivation: cosine *"is suboptimal during training and underestimates the model's performance for the same token count,"* and *"the training duration must be known in advance and match the cosine length"* — fitting scaling laws therefore needs many from-scratch runs of different lengths [established].

So **"early-stopping a cosine run yields a worse model than that same run would have at its scheduled end" is [established]** — but the load-bearing evidence is **LLM-scale (cross-entropy) and ImageNet-scale**. The *mechanism* (mid-cosine LR is still high → not in a settled minimum) is architecture-agnostic [inference], but the *magnitude* on ViT-Tiny Barlow Twins over 1k–100k images is **untested** [open]. Do not state the gap size as if measured in our regime.

## Q2. Is early stopping even compatible with cosine? Why large-scale training abandoned it

The tension is structural, not incidental. At any step t<T of a cosine, the LR is still well above `η_min`, so the model sits in a high-LR, *un-settled* state. Early stopping picks that state. But the SAME compute spent on a cosine *scheduled* to end at t would have annealed and scored higher (Q1). So a naively early-stopped cosine is **dominated** by a correctly-horizoned cosine at equal compute [inference from Chinchilla App. B + Hägele §2].

What well-cited works actually DO:
- **Goyal et al.** (arXiv:1706.02677): ImageNet ResNet-50 in 1h uses a **fixed 90-epoch step-decay** schedule (warmup + ÷10 at ep 30/60/80), run to completion — **no validation early stopping** [established]. Representative of the supervised-vision convention: pick a horizon, run it out.
- **All canonical SSL** (Barlow Twins 2103.03230, MoCo v3 2104.02057, DINO 2104.14294, MAE 2111.06377, SimCLR 2002.05709): warmup + **cosine to a fixed budget end**, **none early-stop** (see `ssl-stopping-and-lr-schedules.md` Q1) [established].
- The modern alternative is to **decouple stop-time from the decay**: **WSD/constant + late cooldown** (Hägele 2405.18392; MiniCPM 2404.06395) lets you stop "any time" because the *cooldown*, not the run length, supplies the anneal — *"a cooldown can also be initiated retrospectively from a checkpoint"* [established].

**Is early-stopping-on-cosine an explicit "methodological mistake" in a primary source? No clean citation found** [open]. The literature's *positive* construction — Chinchilla matching cycle to steps, WSD existing specifically to remove the horizon commitment — implies it, and secondary sources state "avoid early stopping with cosine," but I found **no foundational paper that names "early-stop a cosine" and benchmarks it as an error**. So the strong form is **[inference]**, well-supported but not a direct quote. Honest framing for the thesis: *the field did not so much "ban" it as build schedules (WSD) that make it unnecessary.*

## Q3. Classic early-stopping theory vs the cosine era

Classic early stopping is an **overfitting remedy** for *supervised, labeled* training, and it was conceived under near-constant / mild step-decay LR — not a predefined-horizon cosine.

- **Prechelt 1998, "Early Stopping — But When?"** (Springer LNCS 1524): validation-loss patience criteria; stop when generalization (val) error stops improving. Assumes RPROP/SGD with a roughly stationary LR; the stopping point is chosen *by the data signal*, with **no horizon fixed in advance** [established]. The schedule and the stop are *independent* — which is exactly the property cosine destroys.
- **Goodfellow, Bengio, Courville, Deep Learning §7.8**: early stopping ≈ L2 regularization; the effective constraint on weight magnitude is the product `ε·τ` (learning-rate × steps). Stopping early = small `τ` = strong implicit weight decay [established]. This rationale is fundamentally an **overfitting/capacity-control** argument on a *labeled* objective.
- **Caruana, Lawrence & Giles, NIPS 2000, "Overfitting in Neural Nets"**: excess-capacity nets generalize well *with backprop + early stopping*; early stopping halts the large net when it generalizes comparably to a smaller one [established]. Again: a supervised-overfitting framing.

Why it "fits" the SGD/step-decay era and "sits awkwardly" with cosine [inference]: with constant or step-decay LR, the LR at the stop step is whatever it was — the model is in a usable, near-settled state and the stop time is a free choice. A cosine *encodes the horizon into the LR trajectory itself*, so "where you stop" and "how annealed you are" are coupled; you cannot stop early *and* be annealed. Early stopping presumes they are decoupled. WSD restores that decoupling.

**Does the overfitting rationale even apply to SSL pretraining? Largely no** [inference, supported]. Barlow Twins pretraining has **no labels and no val *classification* loss**; the failure mode is representation **collapse / stagnation**, not label-overfitting, and the SSL loss is a poor quality proxy (see `ssl-pretraining-validation-monitoring.md`). So a kNN/linear-probe "early-stop" signal in SSL is **monitoring for representation quality plateau**, not Prechelt/Goodfellow overfitting control — a different mechanism wearing the same name. The classic theory does **not** transfer cleanly; cite it for *what early stopping classically was*, not as justification for stopping SSL pretraining.

## Q4. Cyclical / averaging alternatives — they argue for *completing* cycles, not truncating

All three sidestep the conflict by letting LR cycles **finish**, then harvesting the *converged* endpoints — the opposite of reading a mid-cosine point:
- **Smith, Cyclical LR** (arXiv:1506.01186): triangular LR bounds; usable models at cycle minima, not arbitrary mid-points [established].
- **Huang et al., Snapshot Ensembles** (arXiv:1704.00109): M *complete* cosine descents; **snapshot taken at the END of each cycle** — *"At the end of each training cycle … the model reaches a local minimum … before raising the learning rate, we take a snapshot"* [established]. Explicitly waits for the anneal to finish before saving.
- **Izmailov et al., SWA** (arXiv:1803.05407): average weights along a constant/cyclical-LR trajectory for flatter optima/better generalization [established].

Relevance: these are direct evidence of the field's *practice* — **you harvest a model when LR is low/at a cycle minimum, never mid-descent.** They reinforce Q1/Q2: a half-decayed cosine point is not treated as a deliverable model anywhere. (As in `epoch-budgets-and-lr-scheduling.md` Q2c, their *re-ascend-and-re-anneal* purpose differs from "find one peak and lock it in," so they are weak templates for the thesis's selection scheme — but strong evidence on *when* a checkpoint is considered good.)

## Q5. Synthesis for the thesis — risk of option A vs option B

The student chooses between **(A) fixed-horizon cosine + best-val checkpoint (+ cooldown-from-best)** and **(B) constant LR + best-val + cooldown**.

- **The specific risk of A is real and grounded** [inference from established Q1]: if "best val" (a noisy kNN probe) argmaxes at some t well before the cosine horizon T, that checkpoint is **un-annealed** — LR there is still high, the model is mid-descent, and by Chinchilla App. B / Hägele §2 it is *strictly worse than a cosine correctly horizoned to t at equal compute*. Selecting and shipping that mid-cosine point is exactly the dominated state of Q2. If A instead *runs the cosine to T and only then probes/cools*, it is just standard fixed-horizon cosine (the canonical baseline) and the early-stopping question is moot — but then you have **not** early-stopped, you have committed a horizon, which is the very thing the data-efficiency thesis wants to avoid pre-committing (variable convergence across 1k–100k splits, prior reports).
- **B cleanly avoids this** [established that B is sound at LLM/MAE scale; transfer to BT/ViT-Tiny untested]: a constant post-warmup LR makes *every* checkpoint a legitimate, similarly-conditioned state, so a val-based stop/selection is *not* confounded by where you are on a decay curve; the **cooldown supplies the anneal after** you decide to stop (Hägele: cooldown initiable retrospectively from a checkpoint). This is the textbook way to make validation-based stopping compatible with eventual annealing.

**Is "naive early stopping on a cosine schedule" *explicitly warned against* in the literature, or is it an inference?** Be honest in the thesis: the **building blocks are [established]** (cosine concentrates quality at its scheduled end; cycle length must match steps; WSD exists to decouple stop-time from anneal); the **conjunction "therefore do not early-stop / select a mid-cosine checkpoint" is [inference]** — well-supported, echoed by secondary sources, but I found **no foundational paper that states it as a named pitfall and measures it** [open]. State it as a reasoned inference from Chinchilla App. B + Hägele §2 + Snapshot-Ensembles practice, **not** as a cited result.

**Bottom line.** The early-stopping-vs-cosine science supports treating **B (constant/WSD + cooldown) as the cleaner way to allow validation-based stopping/selection**, and flags **A's mid-cosine checkpoint selection as the under-annealed, dominated state** the cosine-horizon results predict. Caveats to keep the thesis rigorous: (i) the cosine-horizon evidence is LLM/ImageNet-scale — its quantitative transfer to 1k-image ViT-Tiny Barlow Twins is **[open]**, validate empirically (a single A-vs-B run on one split would settle it locally); (ii) classic early-stopping theory (Q3) is an *overfitting* remedy that does **not** cleanly justify stopping label-free SSL pretraining — the kNN-plateau signal is representation-quality monitoring, a distinct mechanism; (iii) if A is run, only select/cool *at or after* the cosine has substantially annealed, never at a high-LR mid-descent peak.
