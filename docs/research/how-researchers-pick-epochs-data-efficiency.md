# How Researchers Reason About & Justify Epoch Counts — for a DATA-Efficiency SSL Study

*Research-agent report, 2026-06-09. Web-verified against primary sources (some figures via ar5iv HTML mirrors — treat exact decimals as approximate pending PDF confirmation). Companion to `ssl-training-budgets-steps-vs-epochs.md` (El-Nouby constant-iterations, Muennighoff repetition, steps-vs-epochs unit), `epoch-budgets-and-lr-scheduling.md` (how the budget *number* is chosen, WSD/cooldown, rewind-to-best), and `ssl-stopping-and-lr-schedules.md`. Those cover steps/compute and the schedule machinery; **this report covers the gap they leave: how to JUSTIFY an epoch count for a DATA-efficiency claim without early stopping or equal-compute.** Tags: [established] = stated/measured in a primary source; [folklore] = widespread practice, no published justification; [open] = no evidence found.*

**Scope discipline.** The student's framing makes equal-compute / constant-iteration / fixed-step schedules OUT OF SCOPE — those answer the *compute*-efficiency question. This report deliberately does **not** re-recommend El-Nouby's constant-iterations (2112.10740); it asks the orthogonal question: *when N is the variable you study, what is the principled way to set epochs?*

## Q1. How do data-efficiency / small-data papers choose training length, and do they reason about N?

The crucial finding: **the two most relevant careful studies hold OPPOSITE quantities fixed**, and the choice is the methodological fork (Q2).

- **Cole et al. 2022, *When Does Contrastive Visual Representation Learning Work?*** (arXiv:2105.05837, CVPR) — **the closest analogue to this thesis**: SimCLR pretrained on dataset *sizes* {50k…1M} (ImageNet/iNat subsets), evaluated by linear/transfer. They **hold EPOCHS FIXED at 1000 across all sizes** (App. C.2: *"We train with a batch size of 4096 for 1000 epochs"*), with **no adjustment for smaller subsets** and no discussion of the resulting iteration discrepancy [established that they fix epochs; their headline data-quantity result — *"little benefit beyond 500k images; 50k→250k worth >10% top-1"* — is thus measured under fixed-epochs, fewer-updates-on-small-N]. This is direct precedent for **constant epochs across N** in a vision-SSL data-quantity study, but note they never *justify* 1000 as a function of N — it is inherited SimCLR convention applied uniformly.
- **El-Nouby et al. 2021** (arXiv:2112.10740) — the OPPOSITE choice: adapt epochs to **hold iterations constant** across sizes (10% IN → 3000 ep, 1% → 30000 ep), *capped* on the smallest sets; and warn *"pre-training longer than 5k epochs leads to a severe drop"* on Stanford Cars (8,144 imgs) [established]. This is the compute-efficiency convention the student has excluded — included here only to mark the fork.
- **Muennighoff et al. 2023, data-constrained scaling** (arXiv:2305.16264, NeurIPS): reasons in **tokens/compute, not raw epochs**; given budget C and unique data, *jointly* solves for N and D — *"there is no epoch-as-a-function-of-N rule"* stated. Their transferable empirical facts: repeating a fixed corpus for **up to ~4 epochs ≈ fresh data** (8.7B model at 4 epochs finishes only **0.5% higher val loss**); fitted **repetition half-life R\*_D ≈ 15 (≈16 epochs)**, beyond which *"returns diminish extremely fast"* [established for LLM pretraining]. **Transfer to vision/SSL is unverified and the regime is wrong** (LLMs are ~1-epoch, single-pass on tokens; SSL is hundreds of passes with stochastic augmentation acting as fresh data) — do **not** claim the "4-epoch" number transfers to images [open]. The *shape* (a saturating-then-decaying value of repetition) is the only thing worth borrowing as intuition, see Q3b.
- **Gani et al. 2022, *How to Train ViT on Small-scale Datasets?*** (arXiv:2210.07240, BMVC): SSL-init on small sets, but training length is convention, not N-derived [folklore for epochs].

**Verdict Q1:** No paper found *derives* epochs from N. Vision-SSL data-quantity work (Cole) picks one convention (1000) and applies it uniformly; the small-data finetuning work (El-Nouby) instead fixes iterations. Reasoning "epochs as f(N)" is essentially **unattested** [open].

## Q2. Is "epochs" ever the principled unit to hold fixed across N? — the methodological heart

This is a genuine, unresolved methodological fork; be honest that **the field has no consensus.**

- **The data-efficiency argument FOR fixing epochs across N:** epochs = *exposure per sample* = number of times the learner sees each datum. If the research question is *"how good a representation can SSL extract from N images?"*, holding **exposure-per-sample constant** isolates the effect of *which/how-many images you have* from *how hard you train on them*. Fixing iterations instead **confounds** the data-quantity axis with a compute axis (smaller N gets vastly more passes → memorization), which is precisely El-Nouby's degradation regime. **Cole 2022 (2105.05837) embodies the fix-epochs choice** and is the citable precedent [established as practice; the *justification* is implicit, not argued in-paper].
- **The argument AGAINST (and why neither is clean):** fixing epochs means small-N runs get *far fewer gradient updates* (1k imgs × E epochs ≪ 100k imgs × E epochs in steps), so a small-N model may be **undertrained** in optimization terms — you then can't tell "few images" from "few updates." This is the standard learning-curve caveat (fixed epochs ⇒ different total experience across N). **There is no choice that decouples all three of {images, exposure-per-sample, total updates} simultaneously** — they are linked by steps = N·E/batch. You must pick which two to control; the data-efficiency framing says control **images (the x-axis) and exposure-per-sample (epochs)**, and *report* total updates as a derived, varying quantity (exactly what `ssl-training-budgets-steps-vs-epochs.md` already does).
- **Train-to-convergence as the third option:** "train each N until its own probe saturates, report the peak." This is the *cleanest* data-efficiency claim in principle (each N shown at its best) but (a) is operationally early stopping (excluded), and (b) on tiny N the peak is followed by *decline* (Q3b), so "convergence" is ill-defined.

**Verdict Q2:** For a data-efficiency claim, **constant epochs across N is the defensible and precedented (Cole 2022) choice**, *provided* you disclose that total updates vary with N and that small-N runs are therefore not iteration-matched. This is NOT equal-compute (steps differ) — it is equal-*exposure*, which is the right control for the data axis. The honest framing in the paper: "we hold passes-per-image fixed so the x-axis is purely *images*; the alternative (fix iterations) would re-introduce the compute axis we deliberately exclude."

## Q3. Convergence-based reasoning that is NOT early stopping: the one-off saturation ablation

**"Run a length ablation once on a representative split, read off the plateau, then HARDCODE that length for all runs" is a recognized, defensible, and widely-used practice** — and it is explicitly *not* early stopping (no per-run validation-triggered halt; the number is fixed a priori for the main sweep) [established].

- **MoCo v3** (arXiv:2104.02057): measured plateau — ViT-S 72.5→73.4% (300→600 ep), ViT-B 76.5→76.7%; 300 chosen *because the ablation showed saturation* [established].
- **DINO** (arXiv:2104.14294): kNN 70.9→72.8→74.5 at 100/300/800 ep — reports the curve, picks a config from it [established that they report the length curve].
- **SimCLR** (arXiv:2002.05709, §B): 1000 ep *"to ensure convergence"*, post-hoc justified by the longer-is-better-until-flat ablation [established].
- **MAE** (arXiv:2111.06377, §A): the cautionary counterexample — *"have not observed saturation even at 1600 epochs"*; here the budget is a **compute ceiling, not a plateau** [established]. So saturation-reading works for joint-embedding methods (BT family) better than for masked methods.

**This is the student's best citable mechanism.** The recipe "ablate length once on 1–2 representative splits, fix the epoch count, report the ablation as the justification" is exactly what MoCo v3 / DINO / SimCLR do, just at a different scale. It sidesteps both early stopping (the number is fixed, not triggered) and equal-compute (the number is chosen by representation saturation, not a FLOP budget).

## Q3b. Overfitting / repetition limits in SSL — the small-N peak-then-decline

The student's observed **1k-image PEAK-then-DECLINE (even with annealing)** is documented and expected; see `ssl-small-data-overtraining-augmentations.md` for the full mechanism survey. Key points for the epoch decision:

- **El-Nouby 2021** (2112.10740): direct evidence — joint-embedding SSL on tiny sets *degrades* past a point (*">5k epochs → severe drop"*); masked methods resist it [established]. So for BT at 1k images there is a **finite tolerable epoch count above which more passes hurt** — meaning "train longer is safe" (the large-data folklore) is **false** at small N.
- **Muennighoff R\*_D ≈ 15** (2305.16264): the LLM analogue of the same shape (value of repetition saturates, then decays). The *number* doesn't transfer to vision, but it predicts the *qualitative* peak-decline [intuition only; transfer = open].
- **Consequence for Q2/Q3:** a single hardcoded epoch count that is right for 100k (where the curve still rises) will be **past the peak for 1k** (where it declines). This is the central tension: constant-epochs-across-N (Cole's choice) risks *overtraining the smallest splits*. Two honest resolutions: (i) pick the constant E from a saturation ablation **on a SMALL split** (so the smallest N is at-or-before its peak, accepting the largest N is slightly undertrained — conservative for the data-efficiency story since it never *overtrains* anything), or (ii) a mild N-dependent rule (Q5).

## Q4. LR schedule given a fixed epoch budget (no early stop)

**Once epochs are fixed, warmup + cosine-decay-over-the-full-budget is the unambiguous, well-grounded SSL default** [established]:

- **Barlow Twins** (arXiv:2103.03230, Impl. Details — verified): *"train for 1000 epochs… learning rate warm-up period of **10 epochs**, after which we reduce the learning rate by a factor of **1000** using a **cosine decay** schedule"*; LARS, batch 2048 (base LR 0.2 weights / 0.0048 biases, scaled by batch/256). Note: decays to base/1000, **a small floor, not exactly zero** [established].
- **DINO** (`main_dino.py`, verified defaults): `--warmup_epochs 10`, `--lr 5e-4`, `--min_lr 1e-6`, cosine after warmup [established].
- **SimCLR / MoCo v3 / MAE**: all use linear warmup + cosine-to-(near)-zero over the fixed budget [established]. Cosine-to-zero over the full budget is the universal joint-embedding-SSL convention.
- **Critical coupling (from `epoch-budgets-and-lr-scheduling.md`):** the cosine cycle length **must equal the actual training steps** (Chinchilla, arXiv:2203.15556) — a cosine aimed at a horizon you don't reach under-decays and under-trains. So *fixing epochs first, then setting cosine length = those epochs* is exactly the correct, low-risk configuration; it removes the horizon-misspecification pitfall by construction.
- **Data-efficiency nuance:** because steps differ across N under fixed epochs (Q2), set the cosine horizon **per split = that split's step count** (warmup in *epochs*, e.g. 10, converted to steps per split). This keeps every run a correctly-horizoned cosine. Convert warmup to a fraction (BT's 10/1000 = 1% of budget) so it scales sensibly to short small-N runs (10 absolute epochs on a 1k run that only lasts ~50 epochs is too long — use ~1–5% of budget) [extrapolation from BT convention; the 1%-of-budget reading is sound].

**Plain cosine-to-(near)-zero over the fixed budget with ~1–5%-of-budget linear warmup is the well-grounded, simple default the student should use.** No data-efficiency-specific schedule trick is established; horizon-free WSD (prior report's recommendation) is an alternative only if the budget is *unknown* — but the whole point of Q3's saturation-ablation is that you *fix* the budget, so a correctly-horizoned cosine is simplest and best-grounded here.

## Q5. Recommendation for THIS thesis (ViT-Tiny/8 @ 64px, BT, N∈{1k…100k}, linear probe)

Honest top-line: **there is NO clean citable "N → E epochs" rule** [open]. The defensible move is a **disclosed one-off saturation ablation** (Q3) feeding a fixed rule, paired with a correctly-horizoned cosine (Q4). Ranked by groundedness / simplicity / risk:

**Option A (RECOMMENDED) — constant epochs across N, E chosen by a saturation ablation on a SMALL split.** Run a length ablation (e.g. probe-vs-epochs) on 1–2 representative splits *including a small one (1k or 4k)*; pick E at the small split's **plateau/just-before-peak** (Q3b). Apply that **same E to all N** (Cole 2022 precedent, 2105.05837). Pair with per-split cosine-to-near-zero, warmup ≈1–5% of budget (BT convention, 2103.03230). *Grounding:* strongest — fix-epochs-across-N is published practice (Cole), saturation-ablation is published practice (MoCo v3/DINO/SimCLR), cosine+warmup is the BT default. *Why pick E from the SMALL split:* guarantees you never *overtrain* the vulnerable small splits (the peak-decline regime, El-Nouby) — large N merely slightly undertrained, which is conservative and honest for a data-efficiency claim. *Risk:* large-N left on the rising part of its curve (disclose it). *Simplicity:* one number for the whole sweep.

**Option B — mild N-dependent epoch rule (more epochs for smaller N), capped.** The CLAUDE.md heuristic ("more epochs for smaller datasets, keep steps roughly comparable") — but this **slides toward equal-compute** (constant steps ⇒ E ∝ 1/N), which is explicitly out of scope, AND El-Nouby shows it overtrains tiny N. *Only* defensible as a *bounded* rule (e.g. E_small = 2–3× E_large, hard-capped well below the small-N peak), justified as "compensating optimization undertraining at small N without entering the repetition-decay regime." *Grounding:* weak — no paper gives this exact rule [folklore/open]; it is a compromise between the two forks. *Risk:* highest (re-confounds the data axis with compute). Use only if a fixed-E ablation shows small-N is clearly optimization-undertrained.

**Option C — fixed E from a LARGE-split ablation, applied to all N.** Picks E where the *100k* curve plateaus. *Problem:* that E is past the 1k peak (Q3b) → overtrains the smallest, most important splits → systematically *understates* small-N data efficiency. **Not recommended** despite being the naive reading of "saturation ablation."

**Bottom line.** Go with **Option A**: one disclosed saturation ablation on a small split → a single constant E for all N → per-split correctly-horizoned cosine-to-near-zero with ~1–5%-of-budget warmup. This is the simplest design that is (i) entirely built from published practice, (ii) honors the data-efficiency framing (controls exposure-per-sample, x-axis = images), (iii) avoids early stopping (E fixed a priori) and equal-compute (E set by representation saturation, not FLOPs), and (iv) never overtrains the fragile small splits. **The professor-facing honesty requirement:** state explicitly that no citable N→E law exists, that you adopt constant-epochs (Cole 2022) as the exposure-matched control, disclose that total updates therefore vary with N, and present the saturation ablation as the justification for E.
