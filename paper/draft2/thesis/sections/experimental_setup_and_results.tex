\section{Experimental Setup and Results}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Experimental setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\textbf{Pre-training data and splits.}
Pre-training uses Tiny-ImageNet~\cite{le2015tinyimagenet}: 100\,000 training
images, 200 classes, native $64\times64$ resolution
(Figure~\ref{fig:datasets}); labels are never used during pre-training. The
pre-training set sizes are $N \in \{1\mathrm{k}, 2\mathrm{k}, 4\mathrm{k},
8\mathrm{k}, 16\mathrm{k}, 32\mathrm{k}, 64\mathrm{k}, 100\mathrm{k}\}$,
drawn as prefixes of a single shuffled master list (seed 42) that interleaves
one image per class, so every split is exactly class-balanced and smaller
splits are subsets of larger ones. The nesting means a larger split differs
from a smaller one only by the images added, so curve differences are
attributable to $N$ alone.

\textbf{Budgets and validation probe.}
Each split trains a fixed epoch budget of \{1k--8k: 1000, 16k: 600, 32k: 400,
64k: 300, 100k: 250\} epochs, sized from pilot runs to lie generously past
the validation peaks observed there; budgets shrink with $N$ because small
splits peak only after hundreds of epochs, while large splits peak within
tens to hundreds. The budget is therefore a second protocol variable that
varies with $N$ --- total optimizer steps span a $25\times$ range across
the curve --- which the best-within-budget reading is designed to absorb,
and which Sections~\ref{sec:results_decoupling}
and~\ref{sec:limitations} revisit. Table~\ref{tab:splits} reports where
each split actually peaked. The validation probe runs every four epochs on
the three smallest splits, every two epochs on 8k and 16k, and every epoch
from 32k upward, so that larger splits --- which run fewer epochs --- still
receive several hundred probes. The Tiny-ImageNet \texttt{valid} split ---
10\,000 labeled images held out from all pre-training splits --- is
partitioned class-stratified into a 5\,000-image gallery and a 5\,000-image
query set, 25 per class each. Query images are classified by their $k{=}20$
nearest gallery neighbors in CLS-embedding space, with Euclidean distance
and no weighting. The same probe records the effective rank of the query
embeddings (the dimensional-collapse diagnostic of
Section~\ref{sec:results_overtraining}) and, as a logged-only diagnostic
that never influences selection, a $k$NN accuracy on CIFAR-10
(Section~\ref{sec:results_decoupling}).

\textbf{Downstream evaluation.}
The annealed encoder is frozen, and a single linear layer
($192\rightarrow10$) is trained on CIFAR-10~\cite{krizhevsky2009cifar},
resized $32\rightarrow64$ (bilinear) and ImageNet-normalized to match
pre-training statistics. The probe protocol is shared across the five-student
project: SGD with momentum 0.9, no weight decay, batch 512, learning rate
$0.2$ ($0.1 \times \mathrm{batch}/256$, the linear scaling rule), cosine
annealed to zero over 100 epochs, fixed evaluation seed independent of the
pre-training seed. The training transform includes a random horizontal
flip. The reported number is the best test top-1 across the 100 probe
epochs; the shared evaluation script does not yet persist the final-epoch
accuracy, so the size of this inflation is not reported in this draft
(Section~\ref{sec:limitations}).

\textbf{Baselines.}
A random-initialization floor applies the identical probe protocol to an
untrained encoder; it isolates how much of the linear-probe accuracy is
bought by pre-training rather than by the architecture and the probe itself.

\textbf{Seeds, tuning, and provenance.}
Every pre-training run is indexed by code commit, split size, and seed. The
protocol fixes three pre-training seeds (42, 43, 44); this draft reports the
completed seed-42 campaign, and the remaining seeds are in progress
(Section~\ref{sec:limitations}). One hyperparameter was tuned: the learning
rate, by a one-off single-seed ablation on the 8k split --- chosen as a
mid-range size --- at an earlier revision of the training code
(Table~\ref{tab:lr_ablation}). Accuracy rises from $10^{-4}$, which under-trains, to a usable band
at $3$--$7 \times 10^{-4}$, then fails outright at $1.5 \times 10^{-3}$:
that run never improves past its warmup phase and ends with an effective
rank of 8 out of 192. Within the band the accuracy differences are about one
point, while effective rank already declines beyond $3 \times 10^{-4}$. We
picked $5 \times 10^{-4}$ --- mid-band, near-peak effective rank, and the
reference value of DINO at batch 256~\cite{caron2021dino} --- and use this
single rate for all splits. All remaining hyperparameters are fixed by the
shared protocol or by the published recipes of Section~\ref{sec:method}.
All runs execute on one consumer GPU; Section~\ref{sec:discussion} reports
wall-clock costs.

Figure~\ref{fig:curve} shows the data-efficiency curve for the completed
seed-42 campaign; Table~\ref{tab:splits} gives the per-split readings.
Pre-training helps at every $N$: the smallest split already reaches 61.4\%,
19.9 points above the random-initialization floor of 41.5\%, and the largest
reaches 71.5\%, 30.0 points above it. The curve is, however, \emph{not}
monotone in $N$ under our protocol: the 4k and 8k splits score 59.9\% and
59.6\%, below the 1k split trained on a quarter of their data. From 16k
onward accuracy rises steadily. The three largest splits selected their
checkpoint within the final probes of their budget (best epoch 394 of 400,
288 of 300, and 248 of 250) --- their validation signal had not plateaued,
so their curve points are lower bounds under this budget. The dip at
4k--8k coincides with a measurable divergence between the selection signal
and downstream accuracy, which Section~\ref{sec:results_decoupling}
analyzes.

\subsection{At the smallest scale, does Barlow Twins over-train --- and is it collapse?}
\label{sec:results_overtraining}

\begin{figure}[!t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/overtraining.pdf}
  \caption{Over-training on the 1k split (seed 42): in-domain validation
  accuracy (left axis, raw and smoothed) and effective rank (right axis)
  against training step. Validation peaks at step 1888 and declines by 18\%
  relative, while effective rank rises monotonically to 103 of 192: the
  late-training decline is not dimensional collapse.}
  \label{fig:overtraining}
\end{figure}

Yes, it over-trains; no, the decline is not dimensional collapse
(Figure~\ref{fig:overtraining}). On the 1k split, the smoothed validation
accuracy peaks at 10.0\% at step 1888 (epoch 472 of the 1000-epoch budget)
and falls to 8.2\% by the budget end --- a relative drop of 18\%. The
effective rank of the embeddings tells the opposite story: it rises
throughout training, from single digits during warmup to 103 of 192 at the
budget end, and never falls (192 would mean every embedding dimension
carries equal variance; a value near 1, a near-constant output). Whatever degrades the representation late in
training, it is not the variance collapsing into few dimensions. We read
the decline as overfitting to the 1\,000 pre-training images --- an
interpretation; we did not isolate the mechanism, and the constant learning
rate is a candidate alternative (Table~\ref{tab:lr_ablation} shows accuracy
is sensitive to it, and it was tuned on the 8k split, not here). The
best-validation rewind exists for exactly this case: the selected
checkpoint predates the decline by design.

\subsection{Does the in-domain selection signal track downstream accuracy?}
\label{sec:results_decoupling}

Across set sizes, yes; within a training run, increasingly no --- and the
mismatch peaks exactly where the curve dips. The selection signal itself
rises monotonically with $N$ (10.0\% to 25.2\% TI kNN,
Table~\ref{tab:splits}), so $N$ ranks identically under either signal at
the extremes; the disagreement concentrates at 4k--16k.

Figure~\ref{fig:decoupling} quantifies the within-run mismatch using the
logged CIFAR-10 kNN diagnostic, which scores every probed checkpoint of
every run but never influences selection. For each split we compare the
diagnostic at the checkpoint our protocol selects against the diagnostic at
its own (smoothed) peak --- a downstream oracle unavailable to a
downstream-blind protocol. The gap forms an inverted U over $N$: below two
points at 1k and 2k, peaking at 4.7 points at 8k, and vanishing at the
largest splits. Under the oracle the diagnostic improves monotonically with
$N$; under in-domain selection it dips at 4k--8k, mirroring the
linear-probe curve. The 8k split is the extreme case: its in-domain signal
kept improving until epoch 880 (step 28\,160), while its CIFAR diagnostic
had peaked at step 4\,416 --- the selected checkpoint lies 24\,000 steps
past the transfer optimum. These are diagnostic-level numbers; the
linear-probe-level oracle comparison requires probing the diagnostic-peak
checkpoints, which the pipeline now saves (Section~\ref{sec:conclusion}).

\begin{figure}[!htb]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/decoupling.pdf}
  \caption{Cost of downstream-blind selection (seed 42; note the metric is
  the CIFAR-10 kNN \emph{diagnostic}, not the linear probe of
  Figure~\ref{fig:curve}): the diagnostic at the checkpoint selected by the
  in-domain signal versus at its own peak (downstream oracle). Under the
  oracle, more data monotonically helps; under in-domain selection a gap of
  up to 4.7 points opens at the mid splits --- at the diagnostic level, the
  dip of Figure~\ref{fig:curve} coincides with selection-and-budget
  effects, not with the data quantity itself.}
  \label{fig:decoupling}
\end{figure}

Two further observations support reading the dip as a selection-and-budget
effect rather than a property of the data quantity. First, at a matched
step count (the probe nearest step 4\,000, before any divergence), the
diagnostic orders the mid splits normally: 44.9\% (1k), 47.6\% (2k), 52.2\%
(4k), 54.2\% (8k). Second, an earlier three-seed campaign, run with different budgets and with
checkpoints selected by the CIFAR diagnostic itself, reached 64.4\% and
65.7\% mean linear-probe accuracy at 4k and 8k --- 4.5 and 6.1 points above
the downstream-blind protocol at the same splits. That comparison changes two
variables at once (selection signal and budget), so it corroborates rather
than isolates the effect. Our interpretation, labeled as such: with a
generous budget, continued training keeps improving in-domain structure
well past the point where the improvement transfers; at mid $N$ the
in-domain signal rises long enough for the transfer optimum to be left far
behind, while at small $N$ the signal itself declines early (rewinding
selection toward the optimum) and at large $N$ the budget ends first.
