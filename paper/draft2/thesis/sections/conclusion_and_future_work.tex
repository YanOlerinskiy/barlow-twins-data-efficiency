\section{Conclusions and Future Work}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We asked how much downstream accuracy each additional unlabeled
pre-training image buys a Barlow Twins--trained small vision transformer,
and what happens when the images are few. The answer, for one seed within
fixed generous budgets: every amount of pre-training data helps
substantially over a random-initialized encoder (by 19.9 points at 1k
images, 30.0 at 100k), but the value of \emph{additional} data is not
monotone --- the 4k and 8k subsets transfer worse than 1k. The smallest
split over-trains, losing 18\% of its peak validation accuracy with no
sign of dimensional collapse, and the curve's dip coincides with the
largest measured divergence between the in-domain selection signal and
downstream accuracy. The honest takeaway is that at this scale, a
data-efficiency curve is a property of data \emph{and} protocol: at the
level of our diagnostic, the stopping and selection policy left as strong a
mark on the mid-range points as the data quantity did.

Three steps follow directly. The remaining pre-training seeds and the
probe-level oracle comparison (the pipeline now saves the diagnostic-peak
checkpoints) will show how much of the dip survives seed variance and
carries from the diagnostic to the linear probe; re-running one mid-range split
under a shorter budget would test the budget side of the same coin. The
projector-width study --- the Barlow Twins-specific capacity knob, held
fixed here --- is the scoped next extension. Beyond those, a multi-task probe suite would test whether the
conclusions extend past CIFAR-10, and evaluating parameter-efficient
fine-tuning would connect this characterization to the regime where the
family-level data-robustness evidence was
established~\cite{elnouby2021largescale}.
